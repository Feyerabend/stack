
## Philosophical Questions on Language

I have a background in analytic philosophy, and it has strong connections to computing.
Analytical philosophy treats many philosophical problems as problems about language, or at
least as problems that become tractable once language is made precise. Frege, Russell,
Wittgenstein, Carnap and later philosophers were all, in different ways, trying to show that
*confusion in thought* is often *confusion in linguistic structure*. Once you clarify syntax,
semantics, and rules of use, many "deep" metaphysical or epistemological problems either
dissolve or become sharply formulated technical questions.

That is exactly the move that compilers make in computer science. A compiler begins with a
vague stream of characters and progressively imposes structure: tokens, syntax trees, types,
scopes, and finally executable meaning. This mirrors the analytic philosopher's attempt to
move from ordinary language to a regimented, formal language where reasoning becomes
explicit and, in some ways, mechanical.

Noam Chomsky is the natural bridge here. His distinction between:
- syntax (formal structure)
- semantics (meaning)
- pragmatics (use)
is almost a direct continuation of the analytic tradition, but operationalised. What
philosophers discussed abstractly, Chomsky and later computer scientists implemented.
A grammar is no longer just a philosophical object; it is an executable specification.

Read more on the [linguistic](LINGUISTCS.md) aspects.

The idea of a parser is especially revealing. In philosophy, parsing is conceptual:
how do we understand a sentence, what structure do we assign to it, what is its logical form?
In compilers, parsing is literal:
```
source code -> tokens -> parse tree -> abstract syntax tree -> meaning
```
This is Wittgenstein's "logical form" turned into data structures.

Similarly, *type systems* are philosophical. They are ontologies.
They answer questions like:
* What is there?
* What kinds of things exist in this universe?
* Which combinations are meaningful?
* Which expressions are nonsense?

This is almost indistinguishable from a metaphysical project,
except it is precise and enforced by machines.

Analytical philosophy aimed to make reasoning rigorous by formalising language. Compiler
theory shows what happens when that program is taken seriously to its limit. Meaning becomes
something that can be computed. Validity becomes something that can be checked automatically.
Ambiguity becomes a bug.

You could even say:
* Analytical philosophy tried to turn thought into logic.
* Compiler theory succeeded in turning (some) logic into machinery.

Chomsky's generative grammar is the turning point: language is not just described,
it is generated by rules. That is exactly what programming languages do.
They are artificial natural languages with:
- a grammar (syntax)
- a meaning function (semantics)
- a community of users (pragmatics)

The difference is that programming languages enforce their philosophy. Natural language
tolerates ambiguity and contradiction. Programming languages cannot. They are analytic
philosophy under maximal discipline.

So compilers are not just technical artefacts. They are the concrete realisation of a
core analytic idea: that understanding is structure, and that structure can be formal,
explicit, and it even eventually may become mechanically processed.


### Other Philosophies

There is also a practical humility in that. Analytical philosophy is deep, but it is slow.
It is a century of people trying to sharpen concepts that programmers now instantiate
daily without reading Frege or Carnap. So recommending that whole tradition as a prerequisite
would be backwards. The computer already is a philosophical position, whether the
programmer knows it or not.

At the same time, analytical philosophy is not the only place where language was taken seriously.
Phenomenology, hermeneutics, structuralism, post-structuralism, even pragmatism all treat
language as constitutive of reality in different ways. They are (often) further away from compilers
and parsers, but sometimes closer to how humans actually experience meaning.

Analytical philosophy asks:
* What is the structure of a meaningful sentence?
* What are the logical conditions of truth?
* How can ambiguity be eliminated?

Other traditions ask:
* How does meaning arise in lived experience?
* How does language shape what can be thought?
* How does power, culture, and history enter language?

For computing, *both* matter.

Analytical philosophy gives you:
- precision
- formality
- executability
- correctness

Other traditions give you:
- context
- interpretation
- ambiguity
- human limitation

LLMs sit exactly at the fault line between these worlds. They are trained on meaning as use,
not meaning as definition. That is closer to Wittgenstein's later philosophy and to hermeneutics
than to Frege. Yet they are implemented using extremely rigid mathematics and formal systems,
closer to the analytic ideal.

So if analytical philosophy explains why compilers work, other philosophies explain why they
are insufficient as total models of understanding.

You could say:
* Analytical philosophy studies language as structure.
* Other traditions study language as life.
* Computing needs both, whether it admits it or not.

A parser does not care about intention. A human user does.
A type checker enforces consistency. A human tolerates contradiction.
An LLM lives in between: statistical structure trained on lived language.

That is why no single tradition is enough. Analytical philosophy gives you the skeleton.
Other philosophies give you the flesh. And modern AI is trying, awkwardly, to assemble a
body from both.

So the value is not in reading a century of analytic texts, but in seeing that computers
are already philosophical artefacts. They encode answers to:
- what meaning is,
- what correctness is,
- what "understanding" is.

Different philosophies offer different answers. Computing has so far mostly
implemented the analytic ones. LLMs are the first large-scale attempt to operationalise the others.


### LLMs and Language on Computers

Even with LLMs, nothing essential has changed about the interface. It still collapses to language.
The difference is only that the language is no longer strictly formal in the classical compiler sense,
but probabilistic and approximate. Still, it is language that mediates everything.

Historically, the interface was:
```
human -> formal language -> machine
```
Now it is:
```
human -> natural language -> model -> formal language -> machine
```
But the bottleneck is unchanged. Language remains the compression layer between intention and execution.

This is philosophically significant. It suggests that language is not just a tool for communication
between humans, but the fundamental protocol for making thought executable. Even when we "talk" to
machines in English, we are still programming them, only with a softer syntax and weaker guarantees.

LLMs do not remove language; they universalise it. They turn natural language into a kind of
meta-programming language. English becomes a high-level interface that compiles, imperfectly,
into formal operations.

So language becomes the middle layer in a deeper sense:
```
thought
->
language (symbolic interface)
->
formal structure
->
computation
->
action
```
This is almost a Kantian picture. Language is the transcendental condition for making intention
visible to a system that can act on it. Without language, there is no stable interface, only raw signals.

Even multimodal systems do not escape this. Images, sound, gesture, all get translated into latent
symbolic structures. Those structures play the same role syntax trees play in compilers.
They are just learned rather than specified.

So the "collapse to language" is not accidental. It reflects something deep:
- Machines need discrete, structured representations.
- From a formal standpoint humans think in fuzzy, contextual intentions.
- Language is the only known bridge that can be both expressive and structured.

Formal languages were rigid but precise. Natural language was expressive but vague.
LLMs inhabit the space in between. They treat natural language as if it were a formal
language, but with probabilities instead of proofs.

This means we have not escaped the analytic project. We have only softened it.
The core assumption is still:
If it cannot be represented in language-like structure, it cannot be operationalised.

And that is exactly the old analytic thesis:
what can be said clearly is what can be reasoned about clearly.
Now we add: what can be reasoned about clearly is what can be computed.

So language is not just an interface. It is, or has become, the universal compilation
target for intention.





### Projects and Ideas to Explore

Here are some project ideas that depart from the themes in this text, extending the
philosophical connections between language, computing, and AI into practical or
speculative explorations. These are meant to provoke thought, blending analytic rigour
with broader interpretive traditions, and could serve as starting points for coding,
research, or conceptual experiments.

#### 1. *Philosophical Type System in a Toy Language*
   - *Description*: Build a simple programming language (using tools like Python's
     PLY or ANTLR for parsing) that incorporates ontological categories inspired by
     analytic philosophy. For example, define types like "Necessary" (for tautologies
     or constants), "Contingent" (for runtime-dependent values), and "Absurd"
     (for type mismatches that represent logical contradictions). Enforce rules
     where "Absurd" expressions cause compile-time errors, mirroring Wittgenstein's
     idea of nonsense.
   - *Departure*: While the text focuses on existing compilers as realisations of
     analytic ideas, this project flips it by designing a language that explicitly
     embeds philosophical metaphysics, potentially revealing limitations in how
     machines handle "deep" ambiguities.
   - *Something to Think About*: Could such a system detect philosophical paradoxes
     (e.g., Russell's set paradox) at compile time? What happens when you introduce
     probabilistic types from LLMs?

#### 2. *LLM-Powered Hermeneutic Analyser*
   - *Description*: Create a tool using an LLM API to analyse texts from non-analytic
     traditions, such as Heidegger's phenomenology or Derrida's deconstruction.
     Input a philosophical passage, and have the LLM generate "interpretive layers":
     one analytic (breaking it into syntax/semantics), one hermeneutic (exploring
     lived context and historical power dynamics), and one computational (translating
     it into pseudocode or a data structure).
   - *Departure*: The text contrasts analytic structure with "other philosophies"
     like hermeneutics; this project operationalises the "flesh" of interpretation
     via AI, testing if LLMs can bridge the gap between rigid formality and fluid
     human meaning.
   - *Something to Think About*: Do LLMs inherently favour analytic precision due
     to their training, or can they simulate deconstructive play? How? What biases
     emerge when "power and culture" are quantified in embeddings?

#### 3. *Pragmatic Programming Language Extension*
   - *Description*: Extend an existing language (e.g., add a Python library) where
     code execution depends on simulated "pragmatic context"--factors like user intent,
     cultural norms, or even simulated historical events. Use LLMs to infer context
     from comments or runtime inputs, altering semantics accordingly (e.g., a
     function behaves differently in a "formal" vs. "conversational" mode).
   - *Departure*: Building on Chomsky's pragmatics and the text's nod to Wittgenstein's
     later "language games," this departs by making pragmatics enforceable, unlike
     natural languages, but softer than traditional compilers.
   - *Something to Think About*: In a world of context-aware code, how do we debug
     "misunderstandings"? Could this lead to AI systems that tolerate contradictions,
     like humans, without crashing?

#### 4. *Multimodal "Language Collapse" Simulator*
   - *Description*: Develop a simulation (using PyTorch or similar) that takes
     non-linguistic inputs (images, audio) and forces them through a "language
     bottleneck"--converting them to natural language descriptions via models
     like CLIP or Whisper, then recompiling to actions or outputs. Compare
     fidelity loss to pure multimodal processing.
   - *Departure*: The text argues language is the universal bridge; this project
     tests that by quantifying how much is lost or gained when non-verbal data
     is "collapsed" to language, departing into empirical AI research.
   - *Something to Think About*: If language is the "transcendental condition,"
     what intentions are inexpressible without it? Could future AI evolve beyond
     language as the core interface, or is it inescapable?

These projects encourage hands-on experimentation, potentially revealing new tensions
between philosophy and computation. They depart by shifting from reflection to creation,
inviting you to iterate or critique them further.
